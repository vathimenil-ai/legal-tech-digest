## What Changed

### 1. Colorado Court Breaks from SDNY on AI Privilege — Three-Way Split Now Active

**What happened:** In Morgan v. V2X, Inc. (D. Colo., March 30, 2026), a federal court held that litigation materials prepared using consumer AI tools retain work product protection under Rule 26(b)(3), finding that AI-assisted preparation reflects attorney mental impressions.

**What's new:** This is the third federal court to address AI privilege and the first to affirmatively protect AI-assisted work product — directly contradicting the SDNY framework that rejected privilege for AI-generated documents. The previously tracked Heppner/Gilbarco divergence was a two-court tension; this converts it into a three-court split with opposing holdings, materially increasing the probability of appellate or Supreme Court resolution.

**Why it matters:** The split creates an immediate, jurisdiction-dependent compliance problem. Any law firm or legal service provider using AI in litigation preparation must now determine which court's framework governs and document AI usage accordingly. The practical implication is that governed AI workflows need audit trails granular enough to demonstrate *how* AI was used — attorney-directed work product vs. autonomous generation — because privilege outcomes now depend on that distinction. LSPs with configurable, jurisdiction-aware governance architectures gain a concrete procurement advantage; those offering undifferentiated AI tools without audit granularity face exposure in jurisdictions following the SDNY approach. [Sources: 1]

### 2. Q1 2026 AI Sanctions Quantified at $145K — First Aggregate Enforcement Baseline

**What happened:** ComplexDiscovery aggregated Q1 2026 AI sanctions data showing at least $145,000 in penalties for AI-fabricated citations, including a record Oregon penalty and the first substantial federal appellate fine.

**What's new:** This is the first quantitative aggregation of AI sanctions for a defined period. Prior tracking identified the appellate-level escalation qualitatively; the $145K figure establishes a measurable baseline and confirms that sanctions are migrating up the judicial hierarchy with increasing dollar amounts.

**Why it matters:** The financial specificity transforms governance from a risk-management best practice into a cost-avoidance calculation with real numbers. For managed review operators and litigation support providers, the ability to demonstrate that AI outputs have been verified within a governed workflow is now directly tied to avoiding quantifiable financial penalties — penalties that are trending upward and expanding to appellate courts. Combined with the privilege split above, the enforcement landscape now penalizes AI misuse *and* creates uncertainty about AI-related protections, a combination that makes comprehensive governance architectures operationally essential rather than aspirational.
