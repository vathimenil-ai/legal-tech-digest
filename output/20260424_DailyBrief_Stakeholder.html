What Changed

1. Sedona Conference releases practitioner framework for AI-generated evidence

What happened: The Sedona Conference Journal published a structured "Decision Tree for Evaluating AI-Generated Evidence," giving courts and practitioners a step-by-step methodology for assessing AI-generated materials in litigation.

What's new: Until now, courts addressing AI-generated evidence have improvised — each ruling created its own ad hoc test. The Sedona Conference framework is the first authoritative, nationally applicable practice standard for this category. Unlike court rulings (which bind only within a jurisdiction) or legislation (which takes months to implement), Sedona publications influence practice immediately and nationwide. Courts have consistently cited Sedona frameworks in discovery orders for over two decades.

Why it matters: This framework becomes the practical yardstick against which legal teams, opposing counsel, and judges will evaluate how AI-generated evidence was created, preserved, and produced. Legal services providers that handle AI-assisted review or produce AI-generated work product now face a concrete standard — not just an abstract governance aspiration. Providers whose workflows can demonstrate alignment with the Decision Tree gain a defensible position when AI evidence is challenged. Those that cannot demonstrate alignment risk having their AI-assisted work product questioned or excluded. For managed review operators specifically, this is the difference between offering "AI-enhanced review" as a marketing claim and being able to show a court that their process meets the industry's own published standard. The Decision Tree also adds a ninth independent governance mechanism to the rapidly expanding enforcement surface area — alongside judicial doctrine, state procurement mandates, professional guidance, federal consumer protection, state evidentiary rules, private rights of action, comprehensive state AI regulation, and evidentiary weaponization of AI records.
