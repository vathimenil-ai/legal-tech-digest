What Changed

Delaware Court Weaponizes AI Conversation Logs in $250M Commercial Dispute

  • What happened: In Fortis Advisors v. Krafton, the Delaware Court of Chancery issued a post-trial opinion finding that a buyer's AI-generated conversations demonstrated intent to engineer avoidance of a $250M earnout obligation, using those records as the evidentiary foundation for an adverse ruling.

  • What's new: Prior case law — Heppner, Gilbarco, Morgan v. V2X — established that AI outputs are discoverable and may lack privilege protection. That was about exposure. This ruling demonstrates what happens after discovery: AI records wielded as the star witness against the party that created them. The distinction matters — discoverability is a procedural risk that counsel can manage; evidentiary weaponization is a substantive litigation outcome that reshapes how organizations must treat every AI interaction touching legal strategy, deal execution, or business operations.

  • Why it matters: This creates three immediate implications for legal services providers. First, eDiscovery scope expansion: AI conversation logs from tools like ChatGPT, Claude, and Copilot are now a recognized document category requiring preservation, collection, and review in commercial litigation — expanding the data universe on every matter where parties used AI. Second, advisory demand: organizations using AI in M&A execution, litigation strategy, or operational decision-making now face a demonstrated risk that their AI interactions become the opposing party's best evidence — this is the exact scenario Morgan Lewis flagged when extending AI governance to the M&A deal lifecycle, now validated by a court. Third, governance services pull-through: the ruling adds a seventh distinct enforcement mechanism to the governance landscape — not just discoverability risk but affirmative evidentiary weaponization — strengthening the commercial case for proactive AI records management as a recurring service offering. Discovery service providers with AI governance and information management capabilities can position this as both litigation preparedness and risk mitigation. [Sources: 1]
