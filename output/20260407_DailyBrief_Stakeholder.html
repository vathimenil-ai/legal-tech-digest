Bicoastal AI regulation is now law — governance shifts from best practice to legal mandate in the two largest U.S. legal markets

Bottom Line

  • New York's RAISE Act is signed law, effective January 1, 2027. Combined with California's existing executive order, enforceable AI governance requirements now cover the two largest U.S. legal markets. LSPs with mature governance architectures have an 8-month window before this becomes a hard qualification gate; those without face a concrete compliance deadline, not a theoretical one.

  • A third federal court ruled on AI privilege — and this time affirmed work product protection even when public AI tools were used. The emerging judicial message is sharpening: courts will protect governed AI use but punish ungoverned use. This is the clearest signal yet that defensible AI workflow environments are becoming a legal requirement, not a differentiator.

What Changed

1. New York RAISE Act Enacted — Bicoastal AI Regulation Now Binding

What happened: Governor Hochul signed the RAISE Act on March 27, creating detailed obligations for frontier AI model developers, granting rulemaking authority to a new office within the NY Department of Financial Services, effective January 1, 2027.

What's new: This was previously tracked as a bill under amendment aligning with California's SB-53. It is now enacted law. The enforcement mechanism count for AI governance expands to seven — adding state comprehensive regulation with dedicated rulemaking authority to the six mechanisms already documented (judicial doctrine, state procurement, profession-level guidance, federal consumer protection, state evidentiary rules, and state private rights of action). The federal-versus-state governance tension is no longer theoretical: two states representing the nation's largest legal markets have enacted binding AI standards while the White House Framework remains aspirational.

Why it matters: For managed review operators and legal services providers, governance capabilities are transitioning from competitive advantage to table stakes in New York and California. The rulemaking authority is the critical detail — it means implementing regulations with specific technical requirements will follow, and providers with existing auditable governance architectures can absorb those requirements faster than those building from scratch. The January 2027 effective date creates a defined implementation window that rewards incumbency in governance capability. Discovery and managed services providers operating across state lines now face concrete multi-jurisdictional compliance obligations that favor comprehensive governance platforms over point solutions. [Sources: 1]

2. Colorado Court Affirms AI Work Product Protection — Privilege Framework Clarifying

What happened: In Morgan v. V2X, Inc. (D. Colo., March 30, 2026), the court held that AI-assisted litigation materials prepared via public AI tools are protected under Rule 26(b)(3), and that using a consumer AI platform does not automatically waive work product protection.

What's new: This is the third federal court to address AI privilege, following the Heppner/Gilbarco split that generated widespread law firm analysis. Unlike the prior split, this ruling moves toward resolution on a specific dimension: public AI tool use does not equal automatic waiver. The practical effect shifts the governance question from "whether to use AI" to "how to document and govern AI use" — a reframing that directly validates the value of auditable workflow environments.

Why it matters: For LSPs operating governed AI review environments, this ruling strengthens the defensibility argument. If work product protection attaches to AI-assisted materials when proper workflows are followed, the commercial value proposition shifts: governed AI environments are not just operationally superior — they preserve legal protections that ungoverned use may forfeit. Combined with the $145,000 in AI-related sanctions courts imposed in Q1 2026, the judicial posture is crystallizing into a clear binary: governed use is protected, ungoverned use is penalized. LSPs that can demonstrate auditability in their AI workflows are positioned to sell that protection as a concrete risk-mitigation service, not an abstract compliance aspiration. [Sources: 2, 3]
