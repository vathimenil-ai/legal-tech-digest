| **development_type** | Judicial Ruling / Dicta |
| **fact_summary** | A recent Seventh Circuit decision (reported via EDRM/JD Supra) suggests that opposing counsel may have an obligation to identify and flag AI-hallucinated citations submitted by the other side, not just verify their own citations. This potentially extends the AI governance duty from the submitting attorney to all counsel in the proceeding. If adopted as binding precedent, this would: (1) expand the scope of AI citation verification obligations to every party in litigation; (2) create a new category of malpractice exposure for attorneys who fail to catch opposing counsel's AI errors; (3) further increase the value of AI governance and verification tools as a defensive necessity for all litigation participants, not just those using AI. This is incremental but directionally significant — it widens the governance enforcement surface from "users of AI" to "everyone in the courtroom." |
